How to Write a Cover Letter: A Detailed Guide

Choose a Professional Format

Fil a clean, professional one that is easy to read; stick to standard fonts like Arial or Times New Roman. The letter should be formatted the same way as a business letter. Input your name and contact, the date, and then the details of the recipient. A well-flowing cover letter sends the right message concerning how well you pay attention to detail, and a structure will clearly build the tone for the rest of your application. Our website is packed with professional templates you can structure your letter on if you are not quite sure what to do.

Greet the Hiring Manager by Name

If possible, address your cover letter directly to the hiring manager. This just shows that you have done your research, and such a move goes a long way. A custom greeting can set the right tone for the rest of your letter.

Dear Ms. Smith, ...
If you do not know who the hiring manager is, other options include such standard greetings as "Dear Hiring Manager".
Open with a Strong Introduction

The first paragraph should immediately grab attention. Mention the job you're applying for, introduce yourself, and briefly state why you’re excited about the role. Highlight a key skill or experience that makes you a good fit.

I'm applying for the Marketing Specialist position at XYZ Company. With over three years of experience in digital marketing and a passion for branding, I am excited about the opportunity to contribute to your team.
Tailor to the Job Description

Review the job posting carefully and match your skills and experiences with the key requirements. In the body of your letter, emphasize how your background aligns with the company’s needs. Personalization is key, and by tailoring your cover letter to each job posting, you'll significantly improve your chances in the job search.

For example, if the job description calls for project management skills, provide examples from your previous roles where you successfully managed projects.

Cover Letter Layout: What to Include

In this guide, we’ll walk you through the basics, share helpful tips, and provide examples so you can easily create a cover letter that highlights your strengths and gets you noticed.
Body of the Letter

Add your full name, phone number, email address, and current date to ensure that the hiring manager can easily reach you and notice your open disposition for contact.

Also include the contact information of the employer, the name, title with company name, and address.

Greeting/Salutation

Start with a professional greeting, addressing the hiring manager by name if possible. This personal touch shows your attention to detail and respect for the recipient. If the name isn’t available, use a professional greeting such as "Dear Hiring Manager."

“Dear Ms. Smith, ...”
Opening Paragraph

Start with an attention-grabbing introduction. Mention the position you're applying for, how you found it, and a brief statement on why you're excited about the opportunity. Highlight your enthusiasm and key qualifications.

"I am writing to express my interest in the Marketing Specialist position at XYZ Company. With over three years of experience in digital marketing and a proven track record of successful campaigns, I am excited about the opportunity to contribute to your team."
Body of the Letter

Highlight the skills you have and give specific examples of achievements that match the company’s needs. Say something about the company, like one of their values, a recent project they've done, or one of their goals.

"In my previous role at ABC Corp., I led a team that developed a targeted email campaign, resulting in a 20% increase in sales. My skills in market research and strategy development align well with the requirements of this role".
Closing Paragraph

Close with a good closing statement, reiterate your interest in the position, state your qualifications again, and be excited about the upcoming events. Politely ask for an interview or discussion opportunity.

"Thank you for considering my application. I look forward to the opportunity to discuss how my background and skills can contribute to your team."
Sign-Off

Finish with a formal sign-off and your name. This last detail helps keep the cover letter professional.

Proofread and Edit

Before submitting, carefully proofread your letter for any spelling or grammatical errors. A well-edited cover letter reflects your professionalism and attention to detail. Even the best content can be overshadowed by simple mistakes. Taking the time to refine your letter or using an AI-powered tool to help you can make all the difference.

Optional Elements
  • Mention any mutual connections or referrals.
  • Include availability (if applicable).
  • Add a postscript (P.S.) to highlight a key achievement or draw attention to something unique.

Tips for Writing an Effective Cover Letter: Even with No Experience

Writing a Cover Letter Without Experience
When you don’t have direct experience in the field, focus on transferable skills, enthusiasm, and your willingness to learn.
  • Start by acknowledging the lack of formal experience, but pivot quickly to your strengths.
  • Mention skills from school, internships, or projects, like time management and communication.
  • Relate these skills to the requirements of the job.
  • Emphasize your motivation to grow and your strong work ethic.
For instance, if you’re applying for a marketing role, you could mention a personal project where you successfully promoted an event or managed social media accounts. Show that you’ve done research on the company, and explain how your passion for the field aligns with their mission.
Finally, express eagerness to bring fresh perspectives to the team and a desire for mentorship and growth.

Tips for Writing an Effective Cover Letter
1
Keep it short and clear
Your cover letter should fit on one page. Hiring managers don’t have much time, so focus on what matters most by showcasing the key skills and experience relevant to the job.
2
Use strong and active language
Highlight your contributions using verbs like “developed,” “led,” or “achieved.” These words showcase your impact in previous roles.
3
Tailor each cover letter to the specific job
Address the key requirements listed in the job description and explain why your unique skills and experience make you the ideal fit.
4
Let your personality shine through
While maintaining professionalism, give the hiring manager a sense of who you are. This helps you stand out and makes your application more memorable.
5
Use Job-Specific Keywords
Include key terms from the job posting in your cover letter. Many companies use software to screen applications, so using the right keywords can help ensure your letter gets through.
6
Demonstrate Your Research
Reference the company’s projects, values, or goals to show you’ve done your research. This will demonstrate your genuine interest and help you connect your skills to what the company needs.
7
Address Gaps or Career Changes
If you’ve had breaks in your employment or are changing careers, briefly explain the context. Highlight what you learned during that time and how those skills apply to the role you’re applying for.
8
Ask for Feedback
Before sending, ask someone to review your cover letter. They can point out areas where you can improve clarity or tone. A second pair of eyes can catch things you might miss.

A cover letter is a one-page document you submit alongside your resume or CV for your job application.

The main purpose of your cover letter is to:

  • Show your motivation for working at the company
  • Bring special attention to the most important parts of your work history
  • Explain how your work experience fits whatever the company is looking for

What your cover letter is NOT about, is rehashing whatever you already mentioned in your resume. Sure, you should mention the most important bits, but it should NOT be a literal copy-paste.

Keep in mind that the cover letter is usually read after the recruiter scans your resume and decides that you’re qualified for the position.

Your cover letter is just as (if not more) important as your resume.

After all, your resume is what gets your foot through the door, but a cover letter is what opens that door.

So, if you put so much effort into your resume, why not do the same for your cover letter?

A good cover letter template can show the recruiter that you’re serious about the job (especially if it matches your resume design).

Every good cover letter has the following sections:

Header - On top of the cover letter, you write down your own contact information, as well as the recruiter’s (recruiter name, company name, company address, etc…).

Greeting - A formal greeting for the recruiter. E.g. “Dear Sir or Madam,” To learn how to address your cover letter better, check out our article.

Opening paragraph - The introduction of your resume. Here, you summarize your background info (“financial analyst with X+ years of experience”), state your intent (“looking for X position at Company Y”), and summarize your 1-2 achievements to get the recruiter hooked.

Second paragraph - In the second paragraph, you explain how you’re qualified for the position and why the recruiter should pick YOU.

Third paragraph - You talk about why you’re a good match for the company. Do you share common values? Is the company working on projects you’re interested in? Etc…

Formal closing - Finally, you end the cover letter with a quick summary and a call to action (“I’m super excited to work with Company X. Looking forward to hearing from you!”).
